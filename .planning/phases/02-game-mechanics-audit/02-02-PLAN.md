**Section 3: Region Unlock System** (MECH-02, D-05, D-06)

What it does: Five regions unlock as the player accumulates emotion check-ins or visits. Three emotion-gated regions (workshop-glade via inspired, fog-valley via stuck, warm-river via frustrated) and two time-gated regions (observatory-balcony at 7 days, the-long-path at 14 days).

Parameters table:
| Region | Unlock Condition | Current Threshold |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| lantern-clearing | Always available | — |
| workshop-glade | inspired count | 3 |
| fog-valley | stuck count | 3 |
| warm-river | frustrated count | 3 |
| observatory-balcony | days visited | 7 |
| the-long-path | days visited | 14 |

Pillar Impact (use admonitions):
- Understood: RED — Implicitly rewards "difficult" emotions with content; the healthiest emotional state (alright) has no dedicated world space
- Inspired: YELLOW — inspired does unlock workshop-glade, but the mechanic frames emotion as a resource to accumulate
- Less Pressured: YELLOW — Thresholds are low (3 visits) and never expire, reducing pressure; but absence of alright pathway creates subtle pressure to select other emotions

Risks:
- Emotion-gating creates an implicit hierarchy where "difficult" emotions are more valuable to the game than "alright"
- Players who are mostly alright may feel their emotional state is less valued by the world
- Emotion counts accumulate forever with no ceiling or decay — thresholds are one-time gates, not ongoing signals

Remediation (per D-04 format — EACH remediation must have strength indicator, justification, and 3rd party reference):

**Finding 1: Emotion-Gating Bias** (per D-05 — present 2-3 options, defer to Allie)
- Strength: **Strong Recommendation** to address
- Justification: The current mechanic directly conflicts with "Understood" pillar. A player who honestly selects "alright" is mechanically disadvantaged.
- Options for Allie:
  1. **Universal contribution**: Every emotion check-in contributes equally to all region unlocks (e.g., total check-ins >= 3 for each region). Eliminates emotion hierarchy entirely.
  2. **Emotion-to-region remapping**: Keep emotion-specific unlocks but include alright (e.g., alright unlocks a new "quiet-meadow" region). Preserves the narrative connection between emotion and place.
  3. **Hybrid**: Emotion check-ins contribute to their associated region AND to a shared "world energy" pool that also unlocks regions. Both specific and general progress.
- Reference: Sebastian Deterding, "Meaningful Play" (2011) — design choices in games communicate values; mechanics that reward some emotions over others make a statement about which emotions matter. Also see "Designing for Emotion" by Aarron Walter (A Book Apart) on how systems signal which user states are preferred.

**Finding 2: Missing "Alright" Region** (per D-06 — SEPARATE from emotion-gating)
- Strength: **For Discussion**
- Justification: This is a content/narrative gap, not just a mechanics problem. The healthiest emotional state has no dedicated world space. This is a creative decision that needs Allie's input as creative director.
- The question for Allie: What would a world space that reflects "I'm okay today" look and feel like? This doesn't need to be answered now — it's a prompt for future design work.
- Reference: Jane McGonigal, "SuperBetter" (2015) — wellness games that validate the "okay" state alongside difficult states show higher long-term engagement because users don't need to perform distress to feel progress.

**Section 4: Emotion Selection UX** (MECH-03)

What it does: Four emotion chips render in a row. Player taps one to check in. Current order: Stuck, Frustrated, Inspired, Alright.

Parameters: Rendering order is determined by JavaScript object key insertion order in `EMOTION_LABELS`. All four chips share identical styling — no color coding, no icons, no size differences.

Pillar Impact:
- Understood: YELLOW — Equal visual treatment is good, but left-to-right ordering places "difficult" emotions first, creating subtle primacy in Western reading patterns
- Inspired: GREEN — No bias against creative states in visual treatment
- Less Pressured: GREEN — No pressure signals in the UX

Remediation:
- Strength: **Suggestion**
- Justification: The ordering creates a subtle bias, not a strong one. Visual parity is maintained. But making the order intentional (via an explicit array) rather than accidental (via object key order) is a worthwhile improvement.
- Recommendation: Define an explicit `EMOTION_ORDER` array so the rendering order is a conscious design choice, not a side effect. Consider randomizing order per session to eliminate positional bias entirely.
- Reference: Don Norman, "The Design of Everyday Things" — affordances and signifiers in UI communicate priority; position is a signifier whether intended or not.

**Section 5: Quest System** (MECH-04, MECH-09)

What it does: After emotion check-in, the app suggests one micro-quest from the selected emotion's pool. Player taps "Done" when finished.

Parameters:
- Canonical quest pool: 10 quests per emotion (40 total) in `src/data/quests.ts`
- Home screen local copy: 3 quests per emotion (12 total) — a truncated duplicate in the home screen file
- Selection: Pure `Math.random()` with no deduplication or history tracking
- `getAnotherQuest()` exists in `src/data/quests.ts` but is NOT used by the home screen

**Tone Parity** (MECH-04):
All four emotion pools maintain consistent voice and calibration. Stuck/Frustrated quests are as validating as Inspired/Alright. No pool implies failure or demands improvement. Pillar impact: GREEN across all three pillars.

**Quest Pool Exhaustion** (MECH-09):
With pure random selection from the home screen's 3-quest pool, the probability of seeing the same quest twice in consecutive visits is 33%. With the canonical 10-quest pool, expected time to see all quests is approximately 29 visits (coupon collector's problem). The function never errors — it always returns a quest, repeating if necessary.

Pillar Impact:
- Understood: YELLOW — Repeated quests can feel mechanical, especially with 33% repeat rate from the truncated pool
- Inspired: GREEN — Quest content protects creative momentum
- Less Pressured: GREEN — No "you've done them all" state; quests simply recycle

Risks:
- The home screen uses a local 3-quest copy instead of the canonical 40-quest dataset. This appears unintentional.
- 33% repeat rate from 3 quests undermines the feeling of a personalized companion

Remediation:
- Strength: **Strong Recommendation** to fix the truncated quest pool
- Justification: The canonical 40-quest dataset exists and was curated. The home screen bypasses it entirely, using only 3 quests per emotion. Switching to the canonical data is a direct improvement with no tradeoffs.
- Recommendation: Replace the home screen's local `QUESTS` object and `pickQuest()` function with an import of `getRandomQuest()` from `src/data/quests.ts`. This is a Phase 4 code change (architecture refactor), flagged here for awareness.
- Strength: **Suggestion** for future — add basic deduplication (track last 3 shown quests, exclude from next selection)
- Reference: Raph Koster, "A Theory of Fun for Game Design" (2004) — repetition without variation erodes engagement; even small pools benefit from tracking recently shown items.

**Section 6: Fog and Leaf Clearing** (MECH-06, D-11)

What it does: The home screen shows fog wisps and leaves. Player touches them to clear a path. Clearing all fog and leaves triggers the next phase of the ritual (door opens, Aetherling walks in).

Parameters:
| Parameter | Current Value |
|-----------|---------------|
| Fog wisp count | 3 |
| Leaf count | 3 |
| Fog wisp sizes | 65, 60, 55 px |
| Leaf touch radius | 40 px |
| Fog touch radius buffer | 25 px (added to wisp size / 2) |

Current persistence behavior: **Reset** — fog and leaf cleared state is React local state (`useState<Set<number>>`). On app close and reopen, all fog and leaves return. When the user has already checked in today, the cleared state is programmatically restored (all cleared).

Pillar Impact of current reset behavior:
- Understood: GREEN — Consistent ritual experience regardless of when user last opened the app
- Less Pressured: GREEN — No sense that user "missed" clearing yesterday
- Inspired: YELLOW — No visible accumulation of effort in the world

**Three options for Allie to consider** (per D-11):

1. **Reset** (current): Every visit starts fresh. The ritual is the same each day. Pro: No guilt for missing days. Con: The world never remembers your effort.

2. **Persist**: Cleared fog stays cleared across sessions. The world accumulates your clearing work over time. Pro: Visible progress, world feels alive. Con: After enough days, there's nothing left to clear — the ritual loses its physical component.

3. **Hybrid**: Fog resets daily but lighter/fewer than before, showing a trace of past clearing. Pro: Daily ritual preserved while world shows memory. Con: More complex to tune; the "trace" must feel meaningful without feeling like judgment.

(Per D-12: All three modes are implemented in `src/config/game.ts` as `fogPersistence: 'reset' | 'persist' | 'hybrid'`. Allie can switch between them and live with each one before committing.)

Pillar Impact comparison table:
| Mode | Understood | Inspired | Less Pressured |
|------|-----------|----------|----------------|
| Reset | GREEN | YELLOW | GREEN |
| Persist | GREEN | GREEN | YELLOW (missing days visible) |
| Hybrid | GREEN | GREEN | GREEN (if tuned well) |

- Reference: "Ritual Design" concepts from Kursat Ozenc and Margaret Hagan, "Rituals for Work" (2019) — daily rituals benefit from consistent structure (reset) but also from a sense of accumulated meaning (persist). The hybrid approach attempts to balance both.

**Section 7: Visit Tracking and Day Counting** (MECH-05)

What it does: Each calendar day (UTC) the app increments `totalDaysVisited` by 1 on first open. The "Day N" counter displays in the UI. Reopening the app on the same day does not increment.

Parameters:
| Parameter | Current Value |
|-----------|---------------|
| Day boundary | UTC midnight |
| Display format | "Day N" on home screen |
| Inflation protection | Date string comparison (same-day check) |

Pillar Impact:
- Understood: GREEN — Accurately tracks engagement without inflating
- Inspired: GREEN — No impact on creative states
- Less Pressured: YELLOW — The "Day N" counter is visible even before check-in. This is a subtle time signal that can create a sense of accumulated expectation.

Known edge case: UTC date rollover means "today" can change at unintuitive local times. A user in US Eastern timezone sees the day change at 7/8 PM local time.

Remediation:
- Strength: **Suggestion**
- Justification: The "Day N" display is a mild time signal. Not urgent, but worth noting as the app matures.
- Options: (1) Remove "Day N" display entirely, (2) Show it only after check-in, not before, (3) Reframe as "Visit N" to emphasize choice over time
- Reference: BJ Fogg, "Tiny Habits" (2019) — habit-forming apps should avoid "day count" displays that create implicit streak pressure even without formal streak mechanics.

**Section 8: Feedback Lines**

What it does: After quest completion, Aetherling shows a feedback line selected randomly from 5 lines per emotion (20 total).

Parameters:
| Parameter | Current Value |
|-----------|---------------|
| Lines per emotion | 5 |
| Selection method | `Math.random()` (no dedup) |

Pillar Impact: GREEN across all three pillars. Feedback lines maintain Aetherling's voice rules. Tone is warm-under-the-surface, non-performative. No line creates pressure or implies judgment.

Note: With only 5 lines per emotion, repeat probability per visit is 20%. This is acceptable for short, affirming lines (unlike quests, which are action-oriented and feel more repetitive).

**Section 9: The Streak Function** (MECH-08)

What it does: `calculateStreak()` at `lib/storage.ts:145` iterates backward from today through up to 365 days of AsyncStorage entries, counting consecutive days with at least one completed quest. Returns an integer streak count.

Status: **This function is a design pillar violation.** It is flagged for removal in Phase 3 (STOR-03).

Why it violates the pillars:
- The function's existence implies streak tracking is a feature. Even though the result is not displayed anywhere currently, its presence in the codebase creates a pattern that future contributors could surface.
- Streaks are explicitly listed in the project's Out of Scope section as an anti-feature that violates the "Less Pressured" pillar.

Performance concern: In worst case (365 days of history), makes 365 sequential AsyncStorage reads. Not currently a problem (function isn't called in production flow), but would be if surfaced.

Pillar Impact:
- Understood: GREEN (not displayed, so no user impact currently)
- Inspired: GREEN (not displayed)
- Less Pressured: RED — The function's existence in the codebase is a latent violation. Any future contributor who discovers it may assume it's intentional and surface it.

Remediation:
- Strength: **Strong Recommendation** — Remove in Phase 3
- Justification: The function has no consumers and directly contradicts a core design pillar. Its removal is already scheduled (STOR-03). Documenting here for completeness.
- This is not a design discussion — it is a confirmed violation with a scheduled fix.
